Case studies
(1) La Antigua, Veracruz, Mexico (2) San Pedro River, Arizona and Sonora
 
Explanation

Water supply is a complex ecosystem service to spatially model, given that groundwater and surface water are inherently connected but move based on different ways, that controls on hydrology and hydrologic models differ greatly based on spatial and temporal scale and the region of the world of interest to the modeler, and that spatial data are unlikely to currently support modeling at both high spatial and temporal resolutions. Given these limitations, our initial hydrologic models use a yearly time step (which is supported by spatial data on important parameters like precipitation, infiltration, snowmelt, and evapotranspiration). We currently consider only surface water, though the infiltration of surface water into groundwater is a key process that we model. We also use a set of generalized, naïve models to represent sources of surface water (rainfall and snowmelt), sinks of surface water (evapotranspiration, infiltration, and storage behind dams), users of surface water, and the flow of surface water across the landscape (routed using HydroSHEDS data). The long-term intent of our modeling process is to incorporate existing hydrologic models wherever appropriate that will more realistically account for hydrologic processes. Over time this includes modeling groundwater and its movement and use. As spatial data continue to improve, it may also be possible to model water supply at finer temporal scales.
