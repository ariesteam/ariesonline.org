At a glance

Freshwater supply

Sources of surface water include precipitation, snowmelt, springs, and baseflow to rivers. Conversely, sources of groundwater include areas of infiltration or artificial groundwater recharge.

Surface water sinks

Sinks of surface water include areas of evapotranspiration and infiltration. Conversely, sinks of groundwater include springs and areas supporting baseflow to rivers.

Not reinventing the wheel

Modeling fresh water supply needs to take into account the fact that groundwater and surface water are inherently connected but move according to different drivers and pathways. Subsurface water flows are considerably more complex, and are governed factors including geology (i.e., porosity of rock layers) and groundwater elevations.  Instead of reinventing the wheel, for this ecosystem service we plan to integrate our existing operational models with existing hydrologic models wherever appropriate that will realistically account for hydrologic processes.  Over time this includes modeling groundwater and its movement and use.  As spatial data continue to improve, it may also be possible to model water supply at finer temporal scales.

 

 

 

Freshwater supply provision is key to human survival and economic production. This service is increasingly under threat due to overextraction and changes in land use and climate at the local and global scale.

In addition to regionally specific models, a generalized global model of water supply is planned for a future release of ARIES.  This model will use global datasets and provide coarser quality model outputs in the absence of regionally-specific ARIES case studies.

In the field

La Antigua, Veracruz


We developed local water supply models in ARIES for Veracruz, Mexico’s La Antigua River.  These models are intended to be regionally specific but more broadly usable than just the case study region: for instance, the La Antigua models are designed to be applicable for Oaxacan-Veracruz volcanic forests and Gulf-Caribbean mangrove ecosystems. Photo credit: J. Harrison-Cox  


 
San Pedro River, Arizona

The San Pedro River, which flows north from Sonora into Arizona, is internationally recognized for its high biodiversity and ecological significance.  A long history of scientific research in the watershed exists to support ecosystem services research on the watershed. Photo credit: R.Weller/Cochise College.  The  San Pedro models are designed for Chihuahuan and Sonoran deserts, Southwestern Sky Island and Sierra Madre Occidental pine-oak forests.
