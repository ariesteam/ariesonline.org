At a glance

A bundle of services

Because of the strong connections, both demonstrated and hypothesized, between areas of high biodiversity, carbon storage, and erosion control, researchers in many regions are exploring the overlap between areas that provide these services.  ARIES models can help the development of economic incentives for forestry and agricultural practices that reduce erosion while improving carbon sequestration and storage, biodiversity, and other ecosystem services.

Erosion prevention and soil support

Sediment regulation can be classified as either a beneficial or detrimental service, depending on whether the user benefits from or is harmed by sediment delivery.

 

Running the sediment flow model allows the user to map spatial connections between sources of sediment, areas that promote sediment deposition, and users that benefit from or are harmed by sediment delivery.

 

Along with modeling and mapping sources of waterborne sediment, we model regions where sediment deposition occurs, along with users who either value beneficial sediment or would be harmed by either delivery of excess sediment or the presence of excessively turbid waterways. 

 

 

 

Erosion and sedimentation are major global problems that impose a high cost on the functioning of ecosystems (e.g. on coastal and nearshore marine ecosystems) and on economic sectors including agriculture, water supply, electric power generation, and navigation. Natural sediment delivery can be a beneficial process for the provision of certain services such as soil formation in agriculture or wetland formation.

Initial ARIES sediment regulation models have been developed to map sources, sinks, uses, and flows of sediment for the Dominican Republic, Madagascar, and Western Washington.  These models are intended to be regionally specific but more broadly usable than just the case study region. 

In the field

Madagascar


Sedimentation is particularly problematic in Madagascar, where high rates of deforestation and low natural rates of succession have led to high levels of erosion. Excess sedimentation can be particularly damaging to rice fields.

 
Dominican Republic


In the Dominican Republic, sediment loss from intensive agricultural practices is impacting hydroelectric production in parts of the country, impeding development efforts and reducing human well-being.
Puget Sound

In Western Washington, sedimentation is important for both providing beneficial coarse sediments (e.g., gravels) for salmon spawning and for avoided habitat siltation for salmon and other economically beneficial species. Sediment delivery can also have negative impacts on drinking water intakes and recreational use and hydroelectric power generation from reservoirs.
