At a glance

Protection from coastal ecosystems

In our current case studies flood events are expected to raise primarily from tropical storms. Spatial data and process-based models of tropical storm probability can inform probabilities of storm events in ARIES. ARIES models provide quantitative maps of the specific areas in the ecosystems where wave and wind mitigation occurs in different degrees.

Wave mitigation can be provided through engineered shoreline protection or natural ecosystems. Coastal ecosystems providing wave mitigation include mangroves, seagrasses, and coral reefs.  In our models we assume that coastal regions with greater density, health, and area of these ecosystem types would receive greater wave protection than artificial coastal flood protection. Other more specific assumptions refer to wave mitigation effects at different tide levels or at different slopes. As a carrier of potential damage to life and property, wind acts differently than waves, with wind typically slowed by tall and/or dense vegetation.

The models acknowledge that the combined effects of seagrass, coral, and mangrove coastal protection are nonlinear.

 

Beneficiaries of coastal flood protection

As with other modules, ARIES considers the different facets of the provision of costal flood protection. Theoretical sources, sinks (areas where the carries of the ecosystem service, in this case water or wind, are modified by ecosystems or users), and beneficiaries are first mapped. These maps indicate where winds and waves are originated, where areas capable of flood mitigation are located and where potentially vulnerable populations are.  Applying flow algorithms to these initial theoretical states of ecosystem provision, ecosystem modification, and population vulnerability produces a clear quantification of the amounts of service produced by different areas in the ecosystems and actual recipients of different levels of the service.  Different combinations of results are possible, for example by excluding sinks (areas of active flood mitigation), ARIES provides a quantification of possible damages in the absence of mitigation  structures of natural or artificial origin.

 

 

 

Disturbance regulation provided by coastal ecosystems can be seen as the protection of economically valued assets from flooding in coastal zones. Coastal ecosystems, including coral reefs, seagrass, and mangroves can be important in dissipating wind and wave energy that can threaten life and property in coastal zones. The geomorphology, vegetation structure and management of coastal areas can highly and variably influence the degree to which human infrastructure and productive activities can be impacted by flood events resulting from storms, hurricanes, tsunamis etc.

In the field

Madagascar


Eastern Madagascar is highly susceptible to tropical storms coming off the southern Indian Ocean.  Its storms are relatively poorly studied, with limited availability of instrumentation for storms and storm impact measurement versus other tropical storm prone regions in more developed parts of the world. For Madagascar, we identify tropical storm probability as the primary determinant of coastal floods.
