Case Study: Madres de Las Aguas Conservation Area                                                          

At a glance

Primary Partner: University of Vermont

Primary Researchers: Lee Gross and Nathaly Agosto Filion

Completed: 2010

Model:  Sediment Regulation

Practical application

In recent years, the hydroelectric generation provided only 18% of the total demand of energy. Of the rest, more than 80% was supplied with imported petroleum, diesel oil and coal, causing negative environmental repercussions. In addition, the imported petroleum is very expensive for the state. So why does the Dominican Republic not produce more hydroelectric energy? The sedimentation is a factor limiting the hydroelectric generation. Because of the bad management of the soil, erosion is generated, causing deposition of material into the rivers. This reduces the riverbed and the amount of water is diminished. Because of this sedimentation, the Jimenoa hydroelectric power station must be cleaned every month, which reduces its efficiency and its economic profit in generation. A suitable and sustainable use of the land would facilitate the increase in the production of hydroelectric energy and would mean a reduction in costs for the country (GTZ, 2001).

 

ARIES provided a tool to demonstrate to decision makers how shade coffee farms and other land management schemes provide critical sources and sinks of soil sediment regulation to beneficiaries across the landscape. This tool equips local farmers' organizations,  companies purchasing agricultural products from the region, conservation organizations and government agencies with the necessary information to make coordinated decisions that improve overall landscape functionality with minimum levels of investment.  

The Pico Duarte Coffee Region and surrounding Madres de Las Aguas (Mother of Waters) Conservation Area are areas of critical ecological, economic and social importance to the people of the Dominican Republic. In recent years much attention has been paid to the establishment of protected areas for the conservation of biodiversity and ecosystem services (Parks in Peril, 1999). Despite these efforts, protected areas represent less than five percent of this working agricultural landscape traditionally in shade coffee. These shade coffee poly-cultures play an essential role in ensuring ecosystem function to conserve water resources, provide habitat for birds, and provide consumptive resources to households (Perfecto et al., 1996; Vandermeer & Perfecto, 2007; Perfecto & Vandermeer, 2008a; Perfecto & Vandermeer, 2008b; Vandermeer et al., 2008). Farmers recognize the role of their shade coffee in maintaining these types of services, but have struggled to maintain their farms. As across the tropics most Dominican coffee farmers are smallholders, managing only 1-3 hectares of land. Farmers generally depend on family members for labor, face a lack of access to land tenure, and live in a constant state of  poverty (ONAPLAN, 2005). Falling coffee prices, trade liberalization and rising input costs have made life difficult for these small farmers.  Since 2002 these increased economic pressures have led to significant changes in the landscape (Taylor, 2008). More than half of small coffee farms have agglomerated into larger farms of monoculture crops such as chayote squash and beans and pasture for cattle. Changes in the landscape have resulted in environmental degradation threatening the stability of this ecosystem to maintain function and provide services at local, national and international levels.

Steps

I. Sediment Regulation Source Value – Priors

 

1. Slope

A slope layer was provided by Bill Morris with units percent slope. Discretization for slope was  calculated using percentage slope. Percentages were divided into four states at 2, 8 and 30%. 2, 8 and 30% were based upon a prior decision made by the ARIES team. Histogram properties were then used to calculate percentages for each state.

·         Level: under 2%

·         Gently undulating:  btw 3-8%

·         Rolling to Hilly:  9-30%

·         Steeply Dissected to Mountainous:   above 30%

 

2. Soil texture

Determine by global soil texture layer. It was determined that the study area fell entirely within one textual class.

 

3. Hydrologic soils group

Determine by global hydrologic soils layer. It was determined that the study area fell entirely within one soils group.

 

4. Tropical Storm Probability

The Columbia Center for Hazards and Risk Research created a natural disaster hotspots database. We used the Global Flood Hazard Frequency and Distribution data layer and found that the majority of our site processed moderate risk of tropical storms.  The data was presented in deciles.  Our region contained two colors, represented the 5th and 7th deciles, the latter only crossed through our region in a thin a stripe.

 

5. Monthly Precipitation

Average precipitation was based on monthly numbers provided by Weather.com for the nearby city of Santiago.  Numbers were based on a percentage of rainfall amounts for 2009 which fell within the following categories: 

·         Verylow: <50mm

·         Low: 50-70mm

·         Moderate: 70-100mm

·         High: 100-130mm

·         Veryhigh: >130mm

 

6. Vegetation Type

Vegetation Type was determined by the Land Use/Land Cover raster data set provided by Bill Morris. The categories were divided as follows

·         Water, Wetlands and Mangroves: 1%

·         Forest and Shrubland:                    50%

·         Coffee and Cocoa:                              8%

·         Urban and Roads:                               3%

·         Intensive Cropland and Pasture:   38%

 

Percentages for each state were based upon provided land cover data divided by the total land area.

 

7. Successional Stage

Qualitative analysis based on total amount of forest cover.  We know there is no old growth so it was not included as a state. 43% is forested as determined from Land Use/Land Cover data set. The remaining 57% was determined as no succession. Late succession was put at 3% with the remaining 40% divided equally between mid and early.

 

8. Percent Vegetation Cover

Vegetation cover was determined using expert opinion and the vegetation type node.  Expert was researcher Lee Gross at the University of Vermont who spent a year interviewing coffee farmers in the region.

·         Urban and Roads, Intensive Cropland and Pasture were grouped into 40% category and split evenly between low and very low = LowVeg and Verylow

·          Coffee and Cocoa, Water Wetlands and Mangroves were grouped 54% = High and Moderate Vegetation

·           Veryhigh was given a probability of 6% based on expert knowledge of the amount of land under protected status and late successional.

 

Conditional Probabilities

 

1. Node Runoff

Conditional probabilities table is set by “pegging the corners“, so that an instance of Very High Precipitation occurring alongside High Tropical Storm Probability resulted in  the highest probability (100 %) for Very High Runoff while the opposite situation (Very Low Precipitation and Low Tropical Storm Probability) also resulted in a 1 for Very Low Runoff.  The probabilities occurring between these two “corners” describes the logical spectrum of possibilities.

 

2. Soil erodibility

 

3. Vegetative Maturity

Conditional probability table is set by “pegging the corners.” We started by ranking between the three underlying nodes. We determined that the order of importance in regards to vegetative maturity was succession stage, percent vegetation cover and vegetation type. 

  

II.     Soil Sediment Sink Value – Priors

 

Erosion sinks are areas where sediment accumulates as it flows downhill and/or downstream.  We only consider the deposition of sediment in floodplains and reservoirs, as opposed to sediment carried and deposited by overland flow.

 

1.   Stream Gradient

Values were set based on Madagascar soil regulation model for stream gradient in sink. 

>2.86˚, 1.15˚-2.86˚, < 1.15˚ (corresponds to % slope breakpoints of 2%, 5%)

 

Histogram properties were then used to calculate percentages for each state.  

 

75%% fall btw 0-2% stream gradient - low

12.5% fall btw 2-5% stream gradient  - medium

12.5 fall above 5% stream gradient -  high

 

2. Floodplain Vegetation Cover

 

Values based on classified GIS data into 0-20, 20-40, 40-60, 60-80, 80-100 percent & did qualitative analysis.

 

3.    Reservoirs

 

Values of 5% for reservoir presence were set using qualitative analysis via land sat imagery of reservoirs as a percent total of the land area.

 

4.    Levees

 

Presence and absence was determined. Qualitative analysis via land sat imagery was used to determine. No levee was identified therefore a number of 1% presence and 99% absence was set.
